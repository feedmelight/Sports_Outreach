GOOH · Navigation · Final structure

Speak the industry's language, not our own.

A grown-up information architecture for GOOH, anchored in how agencies, brands and media owners actually think about DOOH work. Five top-level destinations. Thirteen sub-items. Every word chosen on purpose.

Ben Leyland · Technology Director, FML
For: Denis, Kiri and the GOOH team
Status: Locked for review
The argument

Users come to GOOH with a job. Meet them there.

Every agency, brand and media owner in DOOH already has a mental model. They think in clients, who give them campaigns, which need creative and media. That's how briefs are written, POs are raised, and reports are filed.

The current nav invents new top-level concepts (Atlas, Sites, Inventory, Formats) that don't map cleanly to that existing language. It asks the user to learn GOOH's internal model before they can do anything.

This proposal does the opposite. It uses the words the industry already uses, in the order users already think in them. The clever bits (the screen network, the AI tooling, the compliance database) sit where they're needed, not at the top of the page.

The result is a sidebar that reads as three layers of work: doing (Clients, Campaigns), thinking (Strategy), running (Settings). Each role has a home. Each item has one job.

The structure

Five destinations. No more.

Home for landing. Clients for who you serve. Campaigns for what you make. Strategy for how you think about it. Settings for how you run it.

Functionality of each menu

What each section actually does.

The job each surface holds and the user it's built for. If a feature doesn't have an obvious home in one of these, it probably shouldn't exist yet.

Home

For: everyone, on login

The dashboard. Recent activity, what's live, what's stuck, what's overdue. A producer's morning view. Should answer "what needs me today" in three seconds.

Clients

For: account leads, brand managers

The home of the client relationship. Inside each client sits an optional Brand layer (for enterprise accounts with sub-brands) and the Brand Kit below that.

  • IdentityPalette, typography, logos, guardrails, brand rules. The visual constitution.
  • TrainingLoRA data, fine-tune sets, style playground. The AI layer that makes the brand reproducible at scale.
  • MonitoringConsistency scoring, jurisdictional strategies, brand drift detection.

Campaigns

For: producers, creatives, ops

The operational core. Three stages in left-to-right workflow order. A campaign is the unit of work that gets briefed, budgeted, made and reported on.

  • CreativeGenerate masters, Composite them together, Adapt to formats and languages, Preview before sign-off.
  • MediaPick the screens. Map view for geographic planning, board view for grid-based selection. Toggle inside one surface.
  • DeployReview per-screen with live render map as a view inside. Ship pushes to all destinations.

Strategy

For: planners, strategists, compliance leads

The thinking layer. Two surfaces, two jobs, two different users. Operational staff may never open this. Senior users live here.

  • Insights → PerformanceWhat happened, what's happening. Cross-campaign data, reach, spend, comparative analysis.
  • Insights → PlanningWhat could happen. Scenario modelling, opportunity mapping, forecasting.
  • Compliance → InventoryThe screen estate. Sites and screens as the physical backbone of the network.
  • Compliance → RulesThe regulatory database. Compliance graph and jurisdictional map. Where you can run what, globally.

Settings

For: admins, billing, IT

Team, billing, API keys, integrations, audit logs. Standard system-level housekeeping. Out of the way until it's needed.

Simplifications made

From 24 sub-items to 13.

Every cut was made on the same principle: a sub-nav item should be a place you go to do a different job, not a different view of the same job. View toggles (map, board, list, live render) live inside each surface, not in the nav.

The language

Do's and don'ts. One word per concept.

This is the lock. Industry language wins over invented language. One word per concept, used consistently across nav, copy, error states, docs and sales. Verbs for actions, nouns for things. If a word isn't on the left, it shouldn't be in the product.

The principle behind every choice: If the industry already has a word for it, we use that word. If the industry uses three words for the same thing, we pick one and lock it. If we've invented a word, we drop it unless it's genuinely better than every alternative.

Why this matters commercially: Every minute a procurement lead spends decoding our vocabulary is a minute they're not buying. Every demo where we have to explain what "Atlas" means is a demo where we look less serious. The product gets sold on clarity, not cleverness.

Locked. Ready to build against.

This is the structure I'd commit to. Five top-level destinations. Thirteen sub-items. One vocabulary across the whole product. Every word and every position made on purpose.

There are two calls worth Denis or Kiri's view before we build against this. First, whether Adapt genuinely covers both format and language work for our users, or whether language is a different enough workflow to deserve its own item. Second, the order of Insights and Compliance inside Strategy. Insights first frames GOOH as a strategic intelligence tool with compliance as a feature. Compliance first frames it as compliance-led with intelligence layered on. I'd argue Insights first.

Everything else I'm confident about. Switch to the gallery tab to see how each of the 57 existing screens maps to the new structure.
