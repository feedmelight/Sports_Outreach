GOOH · Navigation proposal

Speak the industry's language, not our own.

A rethink of GOOH's information architecture, anchored in how agencies, brands and media owners actually think about DOOH work.

Ben Leyland · Technology Director, FML
For: Denis, Kiri and the GOOH team
Status: For discussion
The argument

Users come to GOOH with a job. Meet them there.

Every agency, brand and media owner in DOOH already has a mental model. They think in clients, who give them campaigns, which need creative and media. That's how briefs are written, POs are raised, and reports are filed.

The current nav invents new top-level concepts (Atlas, Sites, Inventory, Formats) that don't map cleanly to that existing language. It asks the user to learn GOOH's internal model before they can do anything.

This proposal does the opposite. It uses the words the industry already uses, in the order users already think in them. The clever bits (the screen network, the AI tooling) sit where they're needed, not at the top of the page.

The structure

One sidebar, three pillars.

Clients, Campaigns and Network. Everything else is either a tool used inside one of those, or system-level (settings, home).

Inside a campaign

Three stages, one job each.

A campaign is the unit of work. Inside it, the user moves through three stages in order. Each stage answers a single question.

Creative

Make it

Produce the master creative file. AI-generated elements, composited together, with brand assets pulled from the client library.

  • Generate · AI elements
  • Composite · build the master
  • Preview · check before sign-off

Media

Place it

Select screens from the Network. Build the media plan. Define which screens this campaign runs on, where and when.

  • Browse Network · pick screens
  • Group into plan
  • Daypart and scheduling

Deploy

Ship it

Auto-adapt the master to each screen's exact specs. Review per screen. Export as video files or push directly to DOOH distribution platforms.

  • Per-screen adaptation
  • Preview in context
  • Export or distribute
Why this works

The structure matches three different users.

Same platform. Same nav. Three different entry points into the work, depending on who you are and what you came to do.

Agency

Brief in hand, media planned.

"We've got the BBH Coca-Cola summer campaign. I need to produce versions for the screens we've already booked."

Enters via Campaigns. Creates new. Pulls in brand assets from the client library. Makes the master. The media plan is already locked, so Deploy is where they spend most of their time.

Brand

Exploring what's possible.

"Show me what our brand could look like on Piccadilly Lights, Times Square, and the Burj Khalifa."

Enters via Network. Browses iconic screens. Picks three. Starts a campaign from inside Network and works backwards to Creative. The screens are the constraint that defines everything.

Media owner

Managing inventory.

"I need to update the specs on my Dubai screens and show a client what their content could look like."

Enters via Network. Their home is their inventory. They rarely touch Campaigns. The platform is a sales and validation tool for them, not a production tool.

The logic, double-checked

Three principles we don't break.

01

Top-level nav is for nouns.

Things that exist in the world. Clients exist. Campaigns exist. Networks of screens exist. Generate, Composite and Preview are verbs, they're things you do, not places you go. They live inside Creative because that's the only place those verbs make sense.

02

Sub-nav appears when context demands it.

The Creative / Media / Deploy sub-stages only show in the sidebar when a campaign is open. This keeps the top-level nav clean and matches how the work actually flows. Open a campaign and the stages reveal themselves.

03

Some tools earn two homes.

Preview appears inside Creative (review the master) and inside Deploy (review per-screen adapted versions). Same word, two contexts. That's correct, because previewing the master and previewing the deployment are genuinely different jobs.

Decisions for Denis and Kiri

Three calls to make.

These shape product positioning, not just UX.

Worth a conversation before we commit to build.

Who is the primary buyer?

Naming the top-level "Campaigns" commits us to speaking the language of agencies and brands. If media owners are the primary commercial target, "Inventory" or "Network" might want top billing instead. The structure works either way, but the emphasis changes.

Is Network read-only or transactional?

Can someone book a screen directly from Network, or does that always happen through a media owner conversation? Catalogue or marketplace. Different product, different business model, different revenue lines.

Do enterprise clients need a brand layer?

Coca-Cola has Coke Zero, Fanta, Sprite. Are those clients, or sub-brands inside Coca-Cola? Proposal says optional brand layer between Client and Campaign. Worth confirming this matches how the big accounts actually want to work.
